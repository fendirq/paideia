The theater smelled like dust and hot wires even when nothing was on. If the front lobby was all fake grandness, backstage was the honest version: black curtains fraying at the edge, ladders with paint on the rungs, spike tape stuck to the floor in bright little X marks like someone had tried to label where panic should stand.

I joined stage crew because I did not get cast in the spring musical. I told people I liked building things. That was partly true. Mostly I liked having somewhere to look during rehearsal besides the faces of the people who had made it through auditions. Mr. B, the technical director, saw through this in about six minutes.

"If you're going to mope," he said, handing me a roll of glow tape, "at least do it with precision."

He talked like that all the time, in lines that sounded tossed off but stayed with you longer than expected. During breaks he taught us how to coil cable so it would not knot itself into a hateful little nest. He showed us why you never slammed a prop door unless the script really needed the sound. He could tell who had entered the booth just by the weight of their footsteps on the metal stairs.

One night a few days before opening, the cue-to-cue rehearsal went sideways. A platform rolled in late. Someone missed a lighting cue. An actor started from the wrong place, then froze in a blue wash. From the booth everything sounded worse: headset static, pages turning too fast, the anxious tap of pencils. I was on sound, finger hovering over a button that needed to land exactly after a line about rain.

"Wait," Mr. B said quietly.

That was all. Not a speech. Not a panic. Just wait.

The line came. I hit the cue. Thunder rolled through the house speakers, low and full and maybe a little too dramatic, like the sky had joined the cast and wanted attention. Everyone reset. The scene moved on.

After rehearsal Mr. B stayed behind to re-label a mess of cords with white tape and a black marker. I helped because I did not want to go home yet. The theater was empty in that special way big rooms are empty, still holding onto the shape of noise. He capped the marker with his teeth and said, "People think performance is what matters. Usually it's timing."

I have remembered that sentence in places with nothing to do with theater. In classrooms. In conversations. In the pause before apologizing. What I wanted from that spring had been visibility, a spotlight, proof. What I got instead was a booth full of switches and a man who treated precision like a form of kindness. Sometimes I think I only miss the version of myself who learned to wait for the line, then press the button.
