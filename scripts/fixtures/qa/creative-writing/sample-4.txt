By six in the morning the thermos was on the table. Green, dented, older than I was. My father filled it before sunrise with coffee so dark it smelled almost burnt, though if you said that out loud he would act offended and say, "That is the smell of being awake." The lid never quite closed on the first try. There was always that small plastic click, then a second firmer one.

I used to think the thermos was embarrassing. It had a peeling sticker on the side from some long-ago hardware store, and the handle squeaked when you lifted it. Other people's parents seemed to own stainless steel bottles with smooth logos. My father's thermos looked weathered. It probably was. He carried it to construction sites, to Saturday errands, to folding chairs at my cousin's soccer games. Once he brought it to a parent-teacher conference and set it on the tiny desk beside my report card like it had also come to discuss my missing homework.

What I remember most is the sound. Not the pouring, though that too. The thermos made a hollow glug, patient and deep, as if the coffee were climbing out of a well. But even more than that, I remember the knock it made when my father set it down on counters. Kitchen table. Dashboard. Workbench. Cement ledge outside the apartment while he searched for his keys. It announced him without trying. Not loud. Just familiar.

There was one winter when the handle finally cracked. He wrapped it in gray electrical tape, layer after layer, until it looked as if the thermos had sprained its wrist. "Temporary," he said, which in my family can mean three days or twelve years. I offered to buy him a new one for Christmas with money from my campus bookstore job. He waved that off.

"This one still knows me," he said.

It is such a ridiculous sentence that I laughed, and he laughed too, but neither of us took it back. Because of course that was the point. The thermos had learned the exact angle of his hand. It fit the cup holder in his truck only if you turned it slightly to the left. The dent near the bottom came from a site in Riverside where, according to family mythology, a ladder slipped and the thermos took the hit like a loyal side character in a movie no one should trust for realism.

I am old enough now to understand that objects become fluent in us. They collect the shape of our routines, our small hungers, the hour we wake, the places we keep returning to. My father still uses that thermos. The tape has darkened. The sticker is almost gone. Some mornings when I visit, I hear the first click and then the second, and the kitchen feels briefly arranged around that sound. Not exactly nostalgic. More like steadied. As if the day, before it rushes off, has put one solid thing on the table.
