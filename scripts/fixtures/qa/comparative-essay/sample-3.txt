Sample 3 - Heroism for the Self and Heroism for the Hall

The Odyssey and Beowulf both celebrate heroic figures, but they do not define heroism in the same moral shape. Odysseus is valuable because he survives, adapts, and finds ways to reconnect himself to household and homeland. Beowulf is valuable because he turns courage into public reputation and binds that reputation to the fate of the hall. What stands out to me is that each poem links the hero to community, but one does so through return and reintegration while the other does so through visible feats of strength. The comparison matters because it shows that "heroism" is not one stable code even within epic literature.

Odysseus is not admirable because he is pure. In fact, he lies, disguises himself, and often wins through strategic indirection. That could make him seem less heroic than Beowulf at first glance. At the same time, the poem keeps presenting those qualities as necessary for a world in which survival depends on reading situations correctly. His goal is not simply personal glory. It is the restoration of a damaged household and a political order threatened by the suitors. In other words, his cunning is not outside the social good. It becomes one way of serving it.

Beowulf works differently because the poem puts much more pressure on public action and public memory. When Beowulf fights Grendel or the dragon, the acts matter partly because they can be seen, sung, and attached to the honor of a people. This matters because courage here is inseparable from reputation. The hero stands forward for the hall, and the poem keeps asking whether there will be anyone capable of doing that after him. Even Beowulf's final victory has a tragic edge, since the cost of his fame is the weakening of the community that depends on him.

Someone could argue that Odysseus is just a more individualistic hero while Beowulf represents a nobler communal ideal. I see the appeal of that distinction, but it feels slightly too neat. Odysseus is deeply tied to home, kinship, and rule, just in a less ceremonial way. Beowulf, meanwhile, cannot escape the loneliness built into exceptional strength. So I would say The Odyssey imagines heroism as intelligent return, whereas Beowulf imagines heroism as glorious expenditure. Both are tied to community, but one sustains it through reentry and the other through sacrifice. That is why Beowulf feels grander, while The Odyssey feels more durable.
