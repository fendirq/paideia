Comparative Essay 2 - Monstrosity, Selfhood, and Responsibility

In our unit on nineteenth-century Gothic fiction, we have seen that the "monster" is rarely just a body. In both Mary Shelley's Frankenstein (1818) and Robert Louis Stevenson's The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde (1886), monstrosity is tied to questions of responsibility, secrecy, sympathy, and self-making. For this essay, compare these two works in order to answer a sharper question than "how are they similar?" Which text offers the more convincing explanation of how monstrosity is produced? Is the monster primarily made by social abandonment and failures of care, or by the subject's attempt to separate unacceptable desires from the rest of the self? You may argue that one framework finally explains both texts better, or that each text exposes the limits of the other. In either case, the comparison should generate an argument with stakes.

A strong essay will not treat the novels as interchangeable examples of "the Gothic." Attend to differences in narrative form and point of view. Frankenstein asks us to move through layered narration, competing acts of testimony, and a creature who becomes legible through speech. Stevenson's novella withholds direct knowledge for much longer and ties horror to reputation, urban secrecy, and confession. Your essay should explain how those formal differences shape each work's theory of monstrosity. You should also consider historical context at a useful level. Shelley and Stevenson are writing in different moments of the nineteenth century, and the comparison should help you say something about changing anxieties around science, respectability, embodiment, and moral responsibility.

Required elements:
- 1400 to 1600 words, with 1500 as the target length
- A specific comparative thesis in the first paragraph
- Close reading of at least two passages from each literary text
- Use of the supplied source packet, including at least one primary-source quotation and at least one analytical/secondary source
- Explicit comparison between primary textual evidence and a secondary interpretive claim
- A counterargument or competing framework that you take seriously before responding to it
- Clear attribution whenever you introduce a quotation or paraphrase

Please approach this as a polished essay for a sophomore- or junior-level literature course rather than as a timed response. I am not looking for plot summary or a list of similarities. By the end of the paper, the reader should understand not only how Shelley and Stevenson differ, but why that difference matters for thinking about monstrosity as a cultural and moral category.
