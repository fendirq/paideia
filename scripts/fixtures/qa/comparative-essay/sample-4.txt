Sample 4 - Fathers, Value, and Misrecognition

King Lear and Death of a Salesman are both driven by fathers who cannot read the people closest to them, but the scale of that failure is very different in each work. Lear begins with a public ritual that turns love into a competition and authority into spectacle. Willy Loman begins in a smaller, more domestic register, yet he also measures worth through a script that distorts everyone around him. What stands out to me is that both plays connect family breakdown to bad habits of valuation. In one case the language is royal and absolute; in the other it is commercial and aspirational. If I had to choose, Miller gives the more socially specific critique, while Shakespeare gives the more devastating one.

Lear's mistake is not just that he picks the wrong daughter. It is that he wants affection translated into a form he can control and rank. This matters because the opening scene turns intimacy into performance before the family has even started speaking honestly. Cordelia's refusal feels shocking partly because it breaks the rules of the staged event. At the same time, Lear's blindness is not merely personal vanity. It grows out of an older model of authority in which command expects verbal confirmation. Once that model cracks, the play keeps exposing how little Lear actually understands about loyalty, need, and dependence.

Willy's blindness is smaller in scale but more recognizable. He keeps interpreting his sons through dreams of likability, success, and business charisma, even when their actual lives do not fit that script at all. In other words, he cannot stop reading family through market language. That is why the play feels so sad rather than simply grand. Willy is not dividing a kingdom, but he is still destroying intimacy by forcing it into the wrong measure. Biff's anger matters because he sees that the false story is not only inaccurate. It is exhausting.

There is an argument that Lear is the more universal play because it strips the problem down to pride, age, and suffering. I think that is partly right. Still, Death of a Salesman makes misrecognition feel historically manufactured in a way that is hard to ignore. Willy has inherited a vocabulary of worth that makes self-deception seem almost normal. So both plays show fathers failing to read their children, but Shakespeare emphasizes the collapse of authority itself, while Miller emphasizes the social myths that make such collapse ordinary. I end up finding Lear bigger, but Miller more precise about why the wrong values keep repeating.
