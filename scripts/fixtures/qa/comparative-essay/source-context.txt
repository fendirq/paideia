--- Source 1: Frankenstein, Chapter 5 / Mary Shelley ---
"It was on a dreary night of November, that I beheld the accomplishment of my toils. With an anxiety that almost amounted to agony, I collected the instruments of life around me, that I might infuse a spark of being into the lifeless thing that lay at my feet. It was already one in the morning; the rain pattered dismally against the panes, and my candle was nearly burnt out, when, by the glimmer of the half-extinguished light, I saw the dull yellow eye of the creature open; it breathed hard, and a convulsive motion agitated its limbs. How can I describe my emotions at this catastrophe, or how delineate the wretch whom with such infinite pains and care I had endeavoured to form? ... the beauty of the dream vanished, and breathless horror and disgust filled my heart."

--- Source 2: Frankenstein, Chapter 10 / Mary Shelley ---
"Remember, that I am thy creature: I ought to be thy Adam; but I am rather the fallen angel, whom thou drivest from joy for no misdeed. Everywhere I see bliss, from which I alone am irrevocably excluded. I was benevolent and good; misery made me a fiend. Make me happy, and I shall again be virtuous. ... I am alone and miserable: man will not associate with me; but one as deformed and horrible as myself would not deny herself to me. My companion must be of the same species, and have the same defects. This being you must create."

--- Source 3: Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Chapter 10 / Robert Louis Stevenson ---
"I learned to recognise the thorough and primitive duality of man; I saw that, of the two natures that contended in the field of my consciousness, even if I could rightly be said to be either, it was only because I was radically both. ... Man is not truly one, but truly two. I say two, because the state of my own knowledge does not pass beyond that point. Others will follow, others will outstrip me on the same lines; and I hazard the guess that man will be ultimately known for a mere polity of multifarious, incongruous and independent denizens. If each, I told myself, could but be housed in separate identities, life would be relieved of all that was unbearable."

--- Source 4: Lecture Packet on Gothic Monstrosity / Course Notes ---
One way to compare Shelley and Stevenson is to ask where each text locates horror. Frankenstein repeatedly turns horror into a failed relationship: a creator refuses responsibility, a social world recoils from unfamiliar embodiment, and the creature learns violence through exclusion as much as through impulse. Stevenson's novella, by contrast, pulls horror inward. It imagines monstrosity not as a being abandoned by others but as a respectable self attempting to split off desire and preserve innocence by administrative means. In that sense, Shelley's monster becomes visible because no one will claim him, while Hyde becomes visible because Jekyll tries to claim only selected parts of himself. Both texts are concerned with accountability, but they organize that accountability differently.

--- Source 5: Seminar Reader, "Sympathy, Secrecy, and the Social Production of the Monster" / Critical Introduction ---
A productive mistake in comparative essays is to assume that Frankenstein and Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde make the same argument in different costumes. The comparison becomes more interesting when we notice that Shelley gives the creature language, grievance, and a theory of his own making, whereas Stevenson delays explanation and lets Hyde circulate first as an effect on observers. Shelley therefore asks the reader to consider whether monstrosity is socially manufactured through abandonment and the refusal of sympathy. Stevenson asks a related but sharper question about bourgeois selfhood: what kinds of violence become possible when a culture of respectability treats desire as something that can be quarantined rather than owned? The monster in Shelley is rejected into existence; the monster in Stevenson is managed into existence.
