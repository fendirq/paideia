Sample 2 - Marriage as Performance and Exit

Pride and Prejudice and A Doll's House both center on marriage, but they do not imagine the institution in the same way. Austen treats marriage as a social structure that can still leave room for judgment, revision, and even a limited kind of mutual respect. Ibsen, by contrast, treats marriage as a performance that looks stable only because Nora has been trained to play a role inside it. What stands out to me is that both texts care about how women are read by others, yet they reach different conclusions about whether intelligent negotiation from within the system is enough. If I had to put it plainly, Austen allows reform within the form of marriage, while Ibsen suggests that the form itself may be the problem.

Elizabeth Bennet matters here because she does not simply reject marriage as such. She rejects bad marriages, foolish marriages, and humiliating marriages. That distinction is important. The novel is critical of social pressure, but it still imagines that character and judgment can improve the institution from inside. Darcy has to change, Elizabeth has to revise some of her first impressions, and the ending depends on both of them learning how not to misread each other. In other words, Austen makes room for critique without imagining total escape. That is one reason the novel feels both sharp and strangely hopeful.

Nora's situation is much tighter. At first, she seems to occupy a comic domestic plot, but the play gradually reveals how little room she actually has to define herself outside the language her husband uses for her. At the same time, Torvald is not only one bad man with a bad personality. He represents a whole structure of patronizing affection that treats dependence as feminine virtue. This matters because Nora's final exit does not read like a personal mood swing. It reads like a refusal of the script itself. The door slam at the end is memorable not just because it is dramatic, but because it marks the collapse of a whole idea of marital harmony.

There is a possible objection that Austen is also quite severe about the market logic behind marriage, and that is true. Charlotte Lucas especially makes that impossible to ignore. Even so, Austen still allows the reader to imagine a good marriage as a repaired version of a flawed system. Ibsen is less willing to grant that comfort. So although both texts expose social performance, Pride and Prejudice finally trusts discernment more than institutions deserve, whereas A Doll's House forces the reader to ask whether discernment matters if the structure itself requires a smaller self. I find Ibsen more radical, but Austen more flexible.
