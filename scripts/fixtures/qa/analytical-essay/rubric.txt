Rubric - Plato's Apology Analytical Essay

Target length: 1400 words. Expected range: 1300 to 1500 words.

An A-range essay for this assignment does all of the following:
- Makes a precise, arguable claim about how Socrates' argument works, not just what he believes
- Stays focused on a single analytical problem instead of drifting into plot summary or background
- Uses close reading to explain why particular words, analogies, and argumentative turns matter
- Engages at least one secondary interpretation in order to sharpen, complicate, or test the main claim
- Integrates quotations with context and explanation rather than dropping them into the paragraph
- Acknowledges an interpretive tension or counterreading and answers it
- Maintains paragraph-level coherence so that each paragraph advances the central claim

Weighted criteria:

1. Thesis and Interpretive Focus - 20 points
- 18 to 20: The introduction presents a specific, debatable thesis about Socrates' argumentative strategy and sets up a clear line of inquiry.
- 14 to 17: The thesis is clear but somewhat broad, descriptive, or only partly analytical.
- 10 to 13: The essay has a topic and opinion, but the claim is vague, obvious, or unstable.
- 0 to 9: The essay lacks a defensible thesis or substitutes summary for argument.

2. Depth of Analysis and Close Reading - 30 points
- 27 to 30: The essay rewards depth over breadth by closely unpacking a limited set of passages, showing how Socrates' language, analogies, and logical sequence produce meaning. Claims are interpreted, not merely asserted.
- 21 to 26: The essay includes real analysis, but some moments are rushed, underdeveloped, or spread too thin across too much material.
- 15 to 20: The essay points to interesting details but often paraphrases instead of analyzing them.
- 0 to 14: The essay remains mostly summary, general reaction, or unsupported claim.

3. Evidence and Source Integration - 20 points
- 18 to 20: Primary and secondary sources are accurately represented, smoothly introduced, and consistently tied back to the thesis. Quotations are brief, purposeful, and unpacked.
- 14 to 17: Evidence is relevant, but explanation is uneven or quotations are not always fully integrated.
- 10 to 13: The essay uses some evidence, but it is thin, misread, or left unexplained.
- 0 to 9: Evidence is minimal, inaccurate, or largely dropped into the essay without interpretation.

4. Engagement with Interpretation and Counterreading - 15 points
- 13 to 15: The essay engages a secondary interpretation or serious counterreading in a way that deepens the analysis rather than interrupting it.
- 10 to 12: The essay acknowledges another interpretation, but the engagement is brief or somewhat formulaic.
- 7 to 9: The essay gestures toward disagreement without developing it.
- 0 to 6: No meaningful engagement with another interpretation or interpretive tension.

5. Organization, Style, and Control - 15 points
- 13 to 15: Paragraphs are ordered logically, transitions clarify the essay's progression, and prose is controlled enough for the argument to remain easy to follow.
- 10 to 12: Organization is mostly clear, though some paragraphs over-explain, repeat, or lose momentum.
- 7 to 9: The structure is uneven, with abrupt shifts, repetitive transitions, or unclear paragraph purpose.
- 0 to 6: Disorganized structure or sentence-level problems make the analysis difficult to follow.

Essays below this level usually lose points for shallow reading, unsupported claims, quote-drops, or trying to cover the whole dialogue instead of closely analyzing one argumentative problem.
