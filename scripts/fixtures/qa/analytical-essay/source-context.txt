APPROVED SOURCE MATERIAL:

--- Source 1: Plato, Apology 28b-29a (translated and lightly modernized for classroom use) ---
"Where a man has once taken his stand, either because he thinks it best or because he was placed there by his commander, there, in my view, he must remain and face danger, without taking account of death or of anything else before disgrace. It would be a strange thing if, after serving where the generals stationed me at Potidaea, Amphipolis, and Delium, I were now, when the god has stationed me to live the life of philosophy, to desert my post because I feared death. To fear death, gentlemen, is really no different from thinking oneself wise when one is not; it is to think one knows what one does not know. No one knows whether death may not be the greatest of blessings for a human being, yet people fear it as if they certainly knew it to be the greatest evil."

--- Source 2: Plato, Apology 29d-30b (translated and lightly modernized for classroom use) ---
"I shall obey the god rather than you, and as long as I draw breath and am able, I shall not stop practicing philosophy and urging any one of you whom I meet. I will say to him in my usual way: 'Best of men, are you not ashamed that you care for money and reputation and honor, but do not care for wisdom and truth and the improvement of your soul?' And if he disputes me and says that he does care, I shall not let him go at once, nor shall I go away; I shall question and examine and test him. If I think he has no virtue, though he says he has, I shall reproach him because he values the less more than the greater. This is what I do to anyone I meet, young or old, citizen or foreigner; for this, be sure, is what the god commands me to do, and I think no greater good has come to the city than this service."

--- Source 3: Plato, Apology 37e-38a (translated and lightly modernized for classroom use) ---
"Perhaps someone may say: 'Are you not ashamed, Socrates, of following a way of life that has now brought you into danger of death?' To him I would make a just reply: 'You are mistaken, sir, if you think a man who is any good at all should take into account the risk of life or death. He should look only to this in his actions: whether what he does is just or unjust, the work of a good or of a bad man.' If you offered to release me on the condition that I no longer spend my life in this search and examination, I would answer: 'Men of Athens, I honor and love you; but I shall obey the god rather than you.' For the unexamined life is not worth living for a human being, and if I say this, you will believe me even less."

--- Source 4: Course Packet Note, "Ignorance as Ethical Method" ---
One line of interpretation argues that Socrates' claim not to know about death is not merely a personal confession of uncertainty. It is an ethical method. By exposing fear of death as a case of pretending to know what one does not know, Socrates creates a standard he can then apply to the rest of civic life. Athenians also act as if they know what success, honor, and virtue are, but their lives show confusion rather than knowledge. On this reading, the death argument matters because it is a compressed model of the entire Socratic project: false certainty produces moral disorder, while acknowledged ignorance opens the possibility of examination. The point is less "death might be good" than "human beings should stop mistaking confidence for wisdom."

--- Source 5: Course Packet Note, "A Tension in Socratic Modesty" ---
Another interpretation pushes back by arguing that Socrates' profession of ignorance is less stable than it first appears. He says he does not know whether death is good or bad, yet he speaks with striking confidence about what justice requires, about the value of philosophical examination, and about a divine mission that authorizes his conduct. That raises a real question for readers: is Socrates consistently exposing false knowledge, or is he replacing one certainty with another? This view does not dismiss the argument, but it suggests that the Apology gains its force partly by turning a modest premise into very ambitious conclusions. A strong analysis should consider whether that expansion feels logically earned, rhetorically strategic, or both.
