APPROVED SOURCE MATERIAL:

--- Source 1: al-Tabari excerpt on the Khurasani movement ---
- al-Tabari describes the movement rallying around "the family of the Prophet."
- The narrative emphasizes black banners, eastern mobilization, and the accumulation of visible support.
- The text frames the movement primarily through legitimate leadership rather than social grievance.

--- Source 2: analytical note on social grievance ---
- Mawali frustration mattered because conversion did not erase older hierarchies in taxation, military prestige, or political access.
- The note argues that the revolution was "a coalition of grievances rather than a single class uprising."
- Social grievance supplied energy, but it was not sufficient without coalition building and Umayyad weakness.

--- Source 3: note on the Battle of the Zab ---
- The Battle of the Zab in 750 was the decisive military defeat that ended Umayyad control in the central caliphate.
- The note stresses that earlier political anger had finally been turned into coordinated force.

--- Source 4: Baghdad and administrative change ---
- Baghdad was founded in 762 under al-Mansur and tied the caliphate more closely to eastern trade routes and Persian administrative culture.
- The early Abbasids expanded the role of viziers and secretaries and relied more visibly on Persian bureaucratic practice.
- The note argues that these changes shifted the empire's center of gravity rather than simply replacing one ruling house.

--- Source 5: historiographical comparison ---
- One modern interpretation argues that the revolution should not be reduced to mawali resentment because it depended equally on Umayyad factional crisis, Khurasani organization, and broad ideological language.
- Another argues that the revolution was social in effect even if it was not socially uniform in origin, because it restructured who could matter within the imperial order.
