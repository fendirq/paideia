English 11 — Analytical Essay Assignment

Due: Friday, May 8 by 11:59 PM (submitted via Google Classroom)
Length: 1,200-1,500 words
Formatting: 12-pt Times New Roman, double-spaced, MLA citations

Prompt:

In The Great Gatsby, F. Scott Fitzgerald presents the American Dream as both an aspiration and an illusion. Choose ONE of the following questions and write an analytical essay that develops a clear thesis supported by textual evidence:

  (A) Is Jay Gatsby a tragic hero, a fraud, or both? Argue for one reading using specific passages from the novel to support your position.

  (B) Analyze the symbolism of the green light across the novel. How does Fitzgerald's use of this symbol evolve from Chapter 1 to the final page, and what does this evolution reveal about his view of the American Dream?

  (C) Compare Gatsby's relationship with Daisy to Nick's role as narrator. How does Nick's perspective shape the reader's sympathy — or lack of sympathy — for Gatsby?

Requirements:
- A clear thesis statement in the introduction that takes a defensible position
- At least 5 direct quotations from the text, each properly cited (Fitzgerald 42)
- Engagement with at least ONE counterargument (e.g., "One might argue... however...")
- A conclusion that synthesizes rather than summarizes
- A Works Cited page (just the novel is fine)

Rubric (100 points total):

  Thesis & Argument (25 pts) — Is the thesis clear, specific, and arguable? Does every body paragraph support it?
  Textual Evidence (25 pts) — Are quotations well-chosen, integrated smoothly, and analyzed (not just dropped in)?
  Counterargument (15 pts) — Is an alternative reading engaged and rebutted with evidence?
  Organization & Flow (15 pts) — Are transitions smooth? Do paragraphs build on each other?
  Style & Mechanics (10 pts) — Clarity, varied sentence structure, grammar, MLA formatting.
  Conclusion (10 pts) — Does it synthesize insight rather than merely restate the thesis?

Tips: Start early. Outline before drafting. Visit office hours if your thesis feels fuzzy — I'd rather help you reshape it now than grade a vague one later.
